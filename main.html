The Annunciation (c. 1473-1476)

Generally thought to be the earliest extant work by Leonardo. The work was traditionally attributed to Verrocchio until 1869. It is now almost universally attributed to Leonardo.

Madonna of the Carnation (c. 1472-1478)

It is generally accepted as a Leonardo, but has some overpainting possibly by a Flemish artist.

The Baptism of Christ (c. 1469-1476)

Painted by Andrea del Verrocchio, with the angel on the left-hand side by Leonardo.[2] It is generally considered that Leonardo also painted much of the background landscape and the torso of Christ. One of Leonardo's earliest extant works. Vasari's statement that the angel on the left is by Leonardo is confirmed by studies by Bode, Seidlitz and Guthman, and accepted by McCurdy, Wasserman and others.

Ginevra de' Benci (c. 1474-1480)

The work was proposed as a Leonardo by Waagen in 1866, and supported by Bode. Early 20th-century scholars were vociferous in their disagreement, but most current critics accept both the authorship and the identity of the sitter.

Saint Jerome in the Wilderness (c. 1480-1482)

Generally accepted as a Leonardo original piece

Madonna Litta (c. 1481-1495)

Traditionally accepted as a Leonardo original piece

Portrait of a Musician (c. 1483-1487)

Widely accepted that Leonardo painted the figure's face. Some scholars suggest the body to be the work of his students, Giovanni Antonio Boltraffio and Giovanni Ambrogio de Predis

Virgin of the Rocks (c. 1483-1493)

Painting Oil on wood panel, transferred to canvas. Unversally accepted as a Leonardo original piece

Lady with an Ermine (c. 1489-1491)

While controversial in the past, modern scholarship widely attributes the work to Leonardo. The attribution of Ginevra de' Benci supports the attribution of this painting

La Belle Ferronniere (c. 1490-1498)

Modern scholars still debate the attribution and it is not as widely accepted as other portraits like Ginevra de' Benci, Portrait of a Musician, and Lady with an Ermine.

The Last Supper (c. 1492-1498)

Universally accepted as a Leanardo original

Sala delle Asse (c. 1497-1499)

Fresco Tempera on plasterodern. Universally accepted as a Leanardo original piece.

Portrait of Isabella d'Este (c. 1499-1500)

Chalk drawing Black and red chalk, yellow pastel chalk on paper. Widely accepted as a Leanardo original piece.

The Virgin and Child with Saint Anne and Saint John the Baptist (c. 1499-1508)

Chalk drawing Charcoal, black and white chalk on tinted paper, mounted on canvas. Universally accepted as a Leanardo original piece.

Salvator Mundi (c. 1499-1510)

Painting Oil on wood panel. Generally accepted Leonardo original piece

The Madonna of the Yarnwinder (c. 1501-1508)

Painting Oil on walnut panel. Generally accepted as Leonardo original piece

Mona Lisa (c. 1502-1516)

Painting Oil on cottonwood (poplar) panel. Universally accepted Leonardo

La Scapigliata (c. 1506-1508)

Painting Earth, amber and white lead on wood panel. Generally accepted as Leonardo original piece

Saint John the Baptist (c. 1507–1516)

Painting Oil on walnut panel. While controversial in the past, modern scholarship widely attributes the work to Leonardo. Scientific evidence in the second half of the 20th century has furthered this attribution.
